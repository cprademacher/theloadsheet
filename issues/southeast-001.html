The Ops Minute
This Week's Topic · Southeast-Specific
The Port of Savannah Effect — And How Southeast Operators Can Profit From It Right Now

Most operators treat the Port of Savannah as a drayage play — something for flatbed or specialized carriers doing port runs. That's too narrow. Savannah's record Q1 2026 means import containers are moving inland at volume — to Atlanta distribution centers, to Charlotte manufacturing hubs, to Tennessee retail warehouses. That inland flow creates the dry van demand that pays $2.89/mile on the Savannah-to-Midwest corridor right now.

The practical implication: if you're regularly running Atlanta to the Northeast, start positioning trucks in the Savannah-to-Atlanta feeder lane. Inland drayage from Savannah to Atlanta isn't glamorous — rates are $1.80–$2.10/mile — but it keeps your truck loaded and positions you perfectly for the Atlanta-to-New York run that pays $3.42/mile. An empty truck deadheading from Florida to Atlanta to catch a good load costs you money. A short drayage run that pays $200–$300 and sets you up for a $1,800 long-haul is smart sequencing.

This is the kind of routing intelligence that separates operators who make $0.35/mile of margin from operators who make $0.55/mile. The difference isn't always finding a better lane — it's connecting your loads so you're never truly empty.

One Thing I'm Watching
◎
Forward Signal · Not A Story Yet
Southeast Capacity Is Tightening Faster Than Any Other Region — Price For It Now

This is the most important structural trend in the Southeast right now and almost nobody is talking about it at the operator level. Ryder's 2026 freight market report specifically flags the Southeast as one of the regions where multiple carrier exits have already caused local capacity tightening — distinct from the national picture, which still looks like an oversupply story.

Translation: at the national level, there are still too many trucks. But in Georgia, Tennessee, and the Carolinas, enough carriers have exited that the local supply/demand balance is already shifting in operators' favor. Tender rejections in the Southeast are running above the national average. Spot rates on key Southeast lanes are outperforming the national spot index by 8–15 cents per mile.

What to do about it this week: stop anchoring your rate quotes to what you charged six months ago. The Southeast is not a national-average market right now. It's tighter than that. If a broker offers you $2.20 on a lane where DAT shows $2.40–$2.50, counter. The market will meet you.
